# Job Statement Patterns

## Bad Examples (and why)

**Solution-specific:**
> "Find a plumber on Angi"
Why bad: tied to a specific product. When Angi dies, the job doesn't.

**Too vague:**
> "Grow my business"
Why bad: not actionable. Every product claims to do this.

**Feature disguised as job:**
> "Get an AI-powered website"
Why bad: describes a solution, not progress the customer wants.

**Segment, not situation:**
> "Small businesses need marketing"
Why bad: "small businesses" is a demographic, not a person in a situation.

## Good Examples (and why)

**Functional + situation:**
> "Answer every inbound call during business hours — even when I'm on a job site"
Why good: verb + object, solution-agnostic, includes context that reveals the constraint.

**With outcome statement:**
> Job: "Follow up on estimates before the customer hires someone else"
> Outcome: "Minimize the time between giving an estimate and sending a follow-up"
Why good: the outcome is measurable and product-agnostic.

**Three-type set:**
> Functional: "Capture every lead that calls, texts, or visits my website"
> Emotional: "Stop worrying that I'm losing money every time my phone rings and I can't answer"
> Social: "Look like a professional operation — not a guy with a truck"
Why good: covers what they do, how they feel, and how they want to be perceived.

## The Format

```
Job: [verb] + [object] + [context]
Outcome: Minimize the [time/likelihood/number] of [negative] when [context]
Importance: [1-10]
Satisfaction with current solution: [1-10]
Opportunity Score: [formula result]
```

## Self-Check

After writing a job statement, verify:
- [ ] Would the customer say this sentence?
- [ ] Is it solution-agnostic? (could be solved by software, a person, or a process)
- [ ] Does it describe progress, not an action?
- [ ] Does it include the situation/context?
- [ ] Could you write a measurable outcome statement for it?
