# Hire/Fire Trigger Patterns

## Bad Examples

**Vague pain list:**
> "Contractors struggle with marketing and lead generation"
Why bad: no specific event, no switching moment, no competing solution named.

**Assumed trigger:**
> "When they realize they need a better website"
Why bad: "realize" is a founder's projection. What ACTUALLY happened?

## Good Examples

**Specific event + competing solution:**
> Hire trigger: "Signs up for Angi during a slow month after a sales rep calls promising qualified leads"
> Fire trigger: "After spending $1,400 and booking only one customer — discovers leads are shared with 3-5 competitors"
> Switch destination: Google LSA, direct SEO, owner's referral network
Why good: names the event, the emotion, the alternative. This is observable, not assumed.

**Timeline format (Moesta):**
> First Thought: "I missed 3 calls yesterday while on a job. One was a $2K estimate."
> Passive Looking: "Started noticing competitors' Google listings look way more professional"
> Active Looking: "Googled 'answering service for contractors' after losing a fourth call in a week"
> Deciding Moment: "Saw the $199/mo price and thought 'that's less than one missed job'"
Why good: maps the actual decision journey, not a rational reconstruction.

## Four Forces in Action

```
PUSH: "I just paid Angi $500 for 7 leads and 4 had bad phone numbers"
PULL: "This thing answers my phone AND knows my services? For $199?"
ANXIETY: "What if it sounds robotic and turns customers off?"
HABIT: "I've always just used my cell phone — it's fine"
```

The skill must surface all four. Missing any one = incomplete picture.

## Self-Check

After writing hire/fire triggers:
- [ ] Is there a specific event (not a gradual awareness)?
- [ ] Is the competing solution named (not "current tools")?
- [ ] Are all four forces represented?
- [ ] Could you find evidence for this in a forum post or interview?
